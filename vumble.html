1. Problem

Redditors don't have an easy way to discover all of the best videos from across Reddit.

History

It started with a frustration when trying to discover videos worth watching on YouTube. Titles and thumbnails are often misleading (clickbait) and YouTube’s recommendations only suggest clips that are similar to what you've already watched.

2. Early Iterations & Research

Our assumption was that short previews would help viewers quickly determine which videos were worth watching.

Powerpoint prototype

First working iteration

Revised viewing experience

Reddit Research

We posted on Reddit and asked the community what they used to discover videos from across Reddit. The general consensus was Reddit.tv (shutdown 1/4/16), but users complained that the service didn't have voting, comments or video submissions. They also mentioned how cumbersome it was to move between videos and comments on Reddit's web experience.

3. Solution

Vumble - web and mobile experience that displays all the best videos from across Reddit in an instantly discoverable feed.

4. Development

I led my team through weekly agile sprints using Waffle.io. We managed our code on Github and deployed to Digital Ocean servers.

Tech Stack

  • Ruby on Rails
  • Video.js
  • Custom Javascript
  • Sass
  • Haml/Erb

Vumble's unique value is its seamless video viewing experience, so reducing friction through front end interactions was key to our success.

5. Feedback & Results

We posted Vumble on Reddit and were inundated with positive remarks. The users continued to suggest features which we used to prioritize our development.

Comment from discussion We revamped RedditTV and it’s just the beginning.

One redditor even posted us on Product Hunt shortly after our launch.

Vumble has received over 150,000 users during its existence with about 1,000 daily active users. It maintains an average session duration above 10 minutes with returning users averaging over 15 minutes per visit.

http://vumble.com – enjoy the experience!
